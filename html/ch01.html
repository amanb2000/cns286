Probability distributions quantify the likelihood of events. Modern language models are chiefly concerned with approximating the probability distribution of sequences of well-formed text. Once a high-quality approximation of the probability distribution of text sequences is learned, text can be generated by sampling the distribution. A basic understanding of probability distributions (Section 1.1) is key to working with large language models. We will then provide an overview of basic methods (ML, MAP) for estimating probability distributions in Section 1.2.

1.1: Probability Spaces

Definition: Probability space – a probability space is a mathematical framework to model random experiments that consists of three elements:

  1. Sample space Ω\Omega: The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment.
  2. Sigma-algebra (ℱ\mathcal F): A collection of subsets of Ω\Omega including Ω\Omega itself and the empty set ⌀\varnothing that satisfies the axioms of σ\sigma-algebras (e.g., closure under complement and countable unions), representing the events or “outcomes” we can assign probabilites to.
  3. Probability measure (PP): A function P:ℱ→[0,1]P: \mathcal F \to [0, 1] that assigns probabilities to events in ℱ\mathcal F.

The probability measure PP must satisfy the Kolmogorov axioms:

  1. Non-negativity: For any event A∈ℱA \in \mathcal F, P(A)≥0P(A) \geq 0
  2. Normalization: P(Ω)=1P(\Omega) = 1.
  3. Countable Additivity: For any countable sequence of pairwise disjoint events {Ai}i=1∞⊂ℱ\{A_i\}_{i=1}^\infty \subset \mathcal F (i.e. Ai∩Aj=⌀A_i \cap A_j = \varnothing for i≠ji\neq j).

These axioms set the stage for deriving important properties of probability distributions. E.g., for any two events A,B∈ℱA, B \in \mathcal F, P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B)P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)

Random Variables

Definition: Random variable – a random variable is a measurable function that maps outcomes from a random experiment to real numbers: X:Ω→ℝX: \Omega \to \mathbb R Random variables quantify random phenomena and are central to probability and statistics.

  • Discrete random variables: Take on countable values (e.g., integers).
  • Continuous random variables: Take on values from an interval or collection of intervals in ℝ\mathbb R.

Notation: Statistics and probability theory might has irritating notation, but we are stuck with it.

  • Uppercase letters (e.g., XX) denote random variables.
  • Lowercase letters (e.g., xx) denote specific values or “realizations” of the random variable XX. We might say xx is sampled from XX or a function of XX as x∼Xx\sim X or x∼g(X)x \sim g(X). You may also see x∼fX(x)x\sim f_X(x) or x∼pX(x)x\sim p_X(x) when attention is being drawn to the probability distribution of random variable XX.

Definition: Support of random variable SXS_X – the support or range of a random variable XX is SX={x∈ℝ∣∃ω∈Ω such that X(ω)=x}S_X = \{x \in \mathbb R \mid \exists \omega \in \Omega \text{ such that } X(\omega) = x\}

1.2 Probability Distributions

A probability distribution describes how probabilities are assigned over the potential values of a random variable.

Discrete Random Variables and Probability Mass Functions (PMFs)

For a discrete random variable XX with support SXS_X, the probability mass function (PMF) pX(x)p_X(x) gives the probability Pr{X=x}\Pr\{X = x\}. PMFs obey the following properties:

  1. Non-negativity: pX(x)≥0p_X(x) \geq 0 ∀\forall x∈SXx \in S_X.
  2. Normalization: ∑x∈SXpX(x)=1\sum_{x\in S_X} p_X(x) = 1
  3. Additivity: For any countable collection of disjoint values {xi}\{x_i\}, Pr(X∈{xi})=∑xipX(xi)\Pr \big( X \in \{x_i\} \big) = \sum_{x_i} p_X(x_i)

Continuous Random Variables and Probability Density Functions

For a continuous random variable XX, the probability density function (PDF) fX(x)f_X(x) is a function such that the probability such that XX falls within interval [a,b][a, b] is given by Pr(a≤X≤b)=∫abfX(x)dx\Pr\big(a \leq X \leq b\big) = \int_a^b f_X(x) dx

Properties of PDFs:

  1. Non-negativity: fX(x)≥0f_X(x) \geq 0 ∀x∈ℝ\forall x \in \mathbb R
  2. Normalization: ∫−∞∞fX(x)dx=1\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x) dx = 1
  3. Probability over intervals: Probabilities are calculated over intervals, not at individual points as applying Pr(a≤X≤b)=∫abfX(x)dx\Pr\big(a \leq X \leq b\big) = \int_a^b f_X(x) dx to an interval of zero length gives zero probability.

The name for the value of the PDF fX(x)f_X(x) at a particular value of xx is called “likelihood” rather than “probability”. If you confuse these, it will anger the statisticians.

Laws of Large Numbers

Recall the definitions of expected value for continuous random variable XX is μ=𝔼[X]=∫−∞∞x⋅fX(x)\mu = \mathbb E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \cdot f_X(x). For XX, we have μ=𝔼[X]=∑x∈SXx⋅pX(x)\mu = \mathbb E[X] = \sum_{x\in S_X} x\cdot p_X(x).

Weak law of large numbers (Khinchin’s law): Let {Xi}i=1n\{X_i\}_{i=1}^n be a sequence of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables with finite expected value μ=𝔼[Xi]\mu = \mathbb E[X_i]. Then, for any ϵ>0\epsilon > 0, limn→∞Pr(|1n∑i=1nXi−μ|≥ϵ)=0 \lim_{n\to \infty} \Pr\begin{pmatrix} \big| \frac 1 n \sum_{i=1}^n X_i - \mu \big| \geq \epsilon \end{pmatrix} = 0

Strong law of large numbers: If {xi}i=1n\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n are sampled from i.i.d. random variables with mean μ=𝔼[Xi]\mu = \mathbb E[X_i], then the probability of the sample mean = μ\mu is 11 as n→∞n \to \infty. Pr(limn→∞1n∑i=1nXi=μ)=1 \Pr\begin{pmatrix} \lim_{n\to \infty} \frac 1 n \sum_{i=1}^n X_i = \mu \end{pmatrix} = 1

Conditional Distributions

Joint distributions for two random variables are denoted as fXY(x,y)f_{XY}(x, y) for continuous random variables and pXY(x,y)p_{XY}(x, y). For brevity, we will refer to each without the subscripts as f(x,y)f(x, y) and p(x,y)p(x, y). Conditional distributions are denoted f(y|x)f(y | x) or p(y|x)p(y | x) and are interpreted as the probability distribution over yy given a known value of xx.

f(y|x)=f(x,y)/f(x) f(y | x) = f(x, y) / f(x)

p(y|x)=p(x,y)/p(x) p(y | x) = p(x, y) / p(x)

Independence: Random variables X,YX, Y are independent iff p(x,y)=p(x)p(y)p(x, y) = p(x) p(y). This also implies that p(y|x)=p(y)p(y | x) = p(y).

Chain Rule of Probability

Let us break free from the assumption of independence in a sequence of random variables {Xi}i=1n\{X_i\}_{i=1}^n. If we know that the process underlying {Xi}\{X_i\} is causal – i.e., XiX_i is conditional only on X1,…,Xi−1X_1, \dots, X_{i-1}, then we can use this handy trick to factor the joint probability of the sequence p(x1,…,xn)p(x_1, \dots, x_n). as

p(x1,…,xn)=∏i=1np(xi|x<i) p(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(x_i | x_{< i})

where x<ix_{< i} denotes x1,…,xi−1x_1, \dots, x_{i-1}. The chain rule of probability is especially handy for modelling language as a random process.

Derivation: By repeatedly applying the chain rule of probability and invoking our causal assumption (XiX_i is only conditional on X<iX_{< i}), we get

p(x1)=p(x1) p(x_1) = p(x_1)

p(x1,x2)=p(x2|x1)p(x1) p(x_1, x_2) = p(x_2 | x_1) p(x_1)

p(x1,x2,x3)=p(x3|x1,x2)p(x1,x2) p(x_1, x_2, x_3) = p(x_3 | x_1, x_2) p(x_1, x_2)

=p(x3|x1,x2)p(x2|x1)p(x1)⏟≡p(x1,x2) = p(x_3 | x_1, x_2) \underbrace{p(x_2 | x_1) p(x_1)}_{\equiv p(x_1, x_2)}

And so on, until we derive the general form.

Note: While I have used discrete/PMF notation p(x1,…,xn)p(x_1, \dots, x_n), the chain rule also applies to causal random processes with continuous variables f(x1,…,xn)f(x_1, \dots, x_n).

1.2: Estimating Probability Distributions

Abstract probability distributions like p(x)p(x), p(y|x)p(y | x), and p(xi|x1,…,xi−1)p(x_i | x_1,\dots, x_{i-1}) are great to think about, but we often are not given the exact form of p(x)p(x) initially. More commonly, we have a bunch of samples from the distribution in question, and we need to estimate p(x)p(x) from samples xi∼p(x)x_i \sim p(x) or xi∼f(x)x_i \sim f(x).

Parameterized probability distributions: We denote the parameters of a probability distribution as θ\theta, and the parameterized distribution as p(x;θ)p(x; \theta) or f(x;θ)f(x; \theta). You may also see the equivalent notation pθ(x)p_{\theta}(x) or fθ(x)f_{\theta}(x) commonly used.

Goal of parameter estimation: Once we have figured out which family of distributions we are interested in, we want to estimate the best parameters θ\theta that explain our measurements.
